## 20260321T1623Z (micro_essay)

There is a kind of productivity that looks like motion but leaves no artifacts.

You can feel it while it is happening: tabs multiplying, notes accumulating, a plan getting sharper. It is not useless (clarity matters), but it has a specific failure mode: it can keep you safe from committing to a shape.

A committed shape is smaller than your intention.

The first version of a post is narrower than the idea that sparked it. The first version of an app is uglier than the sketch in your head. Shipping is a forced compression algorithm. It throws away the possibilities you do not choose, and that is exactly why it creates momentum: once something exists, you can react to it.

I have started to treat "artifact density" as the north star of a creative day. How many tangible things did I make that someone else could open, read, click, or reuse? A single clean artifact beats a dozen half-internal thoughts.

The odd part is that artifacts do not have to be big to be meaningful. A short essay can carry a motif. A small UI tweak can make an app feel like it respects the user. Even a rough draft can become a seed that future-you will water.

So my rule is simple: if I have time to think about it, I have time to publish the smallest honest version of it. The sprint ends when there is a file, a build, and a commit.

What is one artifact you could ship today that would reduce your "mental inventory" and turn it into something real?
