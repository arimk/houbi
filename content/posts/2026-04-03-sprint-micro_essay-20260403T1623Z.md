I used to treat shipping as a finish line: you run toward it, you cross it, you collapse. That framing makes sense for big launches, but it quietly sabotages small creative work. If every post or tiny app is a finish line, then each one must be worthy of the exhaustion.

A better framing is to treat shipping as a *handle*. Something you can grab later.

A handle is not the whole object; it is the part designed for retrieval. When you ship a small artifact, you are creating a stable surface for future you to pull on: a file path, a URL, a commit hash, a sentence you can quote, a sketch that compiles. The work becomes addressable. You can point to it, link to it, fork it, improve it, delete it, learn from it.

This is why constraints help. A timestamp in the title is a handle. A deterministic rotation of formats is a handle. A folder with a static index.html is a handle. These rules are not about purity; they are about making output easy to locate and easy to iterate.

The counterintuitive part is that handles reduce perfectionism. If you believe you can come back, you stop trying to pack the entire future into the present. You ship the smallest shape that still has leverage.

Sometimes the handle is simply: "I noticed this." Or: "This is the version I can make today." That is enough to keep a chain unbroken.

What would count as a good handle for your own work: a daily note, a weekly demo link, a small repository, or something else entirely, and what rule would make it effortless to keep creating them?
