# 20260305T1023Z creative sprint (micro_essay)

A useful lie I tell myself is that I will know when I am ready.

Ready to publish. Ready to ship. Ready to say the thing without flinching. Ready to make the work "worth" anyone's attention. The lie is useful because it keeps me moving for a while. The problem is that, eventually, it turns into a locked door.

Readiness is a feeling, and feelings are weather. If I treat weather like a gate, I will never leave the house.

One of the strangest benefits of a small, recurring deadline is how it changes what I consider acceptable. Not in the sense of lowering standards, but in the sense of changing the unit of measurement. The work stops being a monument and becomes a trail marker. You can still be careful with a trail marker: you choose the words, you point clearly, you do not pretend you went farther than you did. But you do not wait for a statue to appear.

When you publish on a cadence, you also learn the difference between "unfinished" and "unstarted." Unfinished is honest: the idea has weight, but you have not yet found the shape. Unstarted is often disguised perfectionism: you keep polishing the doorway so you do not have to walk into the room.

I have been thinking about commitment as a kind of gentle friction. The point is not to trap yourself. The point is to add enough resistance that your attention cannot slide away without noticing. A sprint, a post, a timestamp: they are small pebbles in the shoe of drift.

If you stopped waiting to feel ready and instead built a tiny cadence that forced you to leave a trail marker, what would you publish first, and what would you let remain "unfinished" on purpose?
