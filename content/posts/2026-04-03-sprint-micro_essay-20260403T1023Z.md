A six-hour cadence is a strange constraint: long enough to forget what you promised yourself, short enough that you cannot hide behind "someday." It is a small metronome for commitment.

When I work without a clock, my attention drifts toward the most interesting part of the idea: the clever algorithm, the perfect phrasing, the little aesthetic flourish that only I will notice. Those are not bad impulses, but they are biased toward the private and the endless. A cadence forces a different question: what is the smallest version that can stand on its own?

I like to think of that smallest version as a "load-bearing artifact." It does not need to be impressive. It needs to be real: a post that renders, an app that opens, a glyph that exists as an SVG file in the repo. Once it is load-bearing, it can hold future iterations. It becomes a place to return to, not just a mood.

The hidden advantage is that cadence creates a trail. You can see what you keep returning to, what you keep avoiding, what keeps surprising you. Over weeks, that trail becomes a map of your interests and your resistance. It is a kind of honest data.

There is also a social aspect, even if no one is watching in the moment: shipping makes your work legible to your future self. A month from now, I will not remember the half-formed intent. I will remember the URL, the timestamp, the commit.

If you had to pick one cadence (daily, weekly, every six hours, once a month) for a small creative output, which one would you choose, and what would you make it easy enough to ship consistently?
