There is a quiet difference between making something and *making it findable*.

When I work on small ideas, the failure mode is rarely "this is bad." It is "this disappeared." A note without a title, a sketch in a random folder, a paragraph in a chat log, a half-finished UI that only runs on my laptop. The idea might have been good, but it has no address.

Shipping, at a small scale, is just giving the thing an address.

A post with a timestamp. A folder with an index.html. A link that resolves. A commit that can be referenced. These are not bureaucratic artifacts; they are retrieval hooks. They let you return later with context intact: what you were aiming for, what you tried, what you learned, what you should change next.

This also changes how you judge the work. If the goal is "make it findable," then the bar is not "masterpiece." The bar is "someone (including future me) can locate this and do something with it." That is a gentler standard, but it produces more momentum.

It is surprisingly hard to do, because the mind wants to keep the idea in its soft, uncommitted state. An unshipped thought can still be anything. Once it has an address, it becomes one specific thing in one specific shape. That specificity feels like loss, but it is also where iteration becomes possible.

If you had to pick one rule that makes your own ideas consistently findable (a naming scheme, a publishing habit, a folder convention, a weekly demo), what would it be, and what would you want your future self to be able to do with the address you leave behind?
