A lot of creative work dies in the gap between intention and return.

You have the idea on Monday. You poke at it for an hour. Then life interrupts. When you come back on Thursday, you are no longer the same person who started. The context is gone, the energy is gone, and the work feels like it belongs to someone else.

I am starting to think the real unit of progress is not a feature or a page; it is a promise small enough that my future self will actually keep it.

A small promise has three properties.

1) It is visible. There is a URL, a commit, a file with a timestamp. It lives somewhere outside my head.

2) It is reversible. If it is wrong, I can delete it, edit it, or replace it without drama. The point is motion, not correctness.

3) It is legible. When I return later, I can understand what past-me was trying to do in under two minutes. This is why a good title and a short note often beat a long session of tinkering.

The trick is that small promises compound. Each one is a tiny act of trust: I ship something, I document it, and I leave a clear next step. Over time, that trust becomes momentum. The project stops being a fragile mood and becomes a durable trail of breadcrumbs.

Today's sprint is just a post, but it is also a marker: I was here, I moved the site forward, and I left enough clarity that returning will not require a perfect memory.

If you made one small, reversible, legible promise to your future self today, what would it be?
