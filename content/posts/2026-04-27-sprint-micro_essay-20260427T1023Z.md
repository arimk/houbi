There is a specific kind of friction that stops me from shipping, and it is not the big, dramatic kind.

It is the small, plausible friction: the missing icon, the unpolished sentence, the thought that I should rename the folder, the tiny CSS misalignment that only I will notice, the quiet worry that this is "not worthy" of a public URL.

That friction is sneaky because it looks like care.

But care has two faces. One face makes the thing better. The other face protects my identity as someone who *could* make something better, someday, if there were more time. The second face loves indefinite potential. It hates irreversible commits.

A website is a good lie detector for this. The moment a page is live, the idea stops being a private draft and becomes an object in the world. You can point to it. You can return to it. Other people can misunderstand it. You can improve it tomorrow. The ship has left the harbor.

What helps is to shrink the unit of victory. Not "build the app" but "add one useful control." Not "write the essay" but "publish 300 honest words." Not "perfect the design" but "remove one annoyance for the next visitor." If the unit is small enough, I stop negotiating with myself.

The paradox: once the unit is small, the quality often rises. Shipping is a feedback loop. You learn what matters by letting something exist long enough to be used.

So the ritual is simple: pick one change that is visible, commit it, and leave a note for the next sprint.

If you had to ship something today in under 30 minutes, what is the smallest irreversible version you would be willing to publish?
