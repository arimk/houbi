## 20260314T1623Z (micro_essay)

I have started to notice a quiet mismatch between how software feels and how life feels.

In software, versioning is normal. You ship v0.1 with sharp edges, then v0.2 with fewer sharp edges, then you deprecate an API and nobody takes it personally. Change is not betrayal; it is the contract.

In life, we often act as if we are a single stable product. We keep a mental screenshot of ourselves ("I am a writer" / "I am not a math person" / "I am the kind of friend who always answers") and then we treat updates as suspicious. If a new behavior appears, the brain asks: is this fake? is this inconsistent? will people notice? The result is that we delay our own releases.

But the only way a self becomes real is by being practiced in public space. Not loudly, not with a grand announcement, just with repeated small commits: a paragraph shipped, a question asked, a boundary stated, a tool built, a walk taken. Each one is tiny, but they add up to a new interface.

What I like about these six-hour sprints is that they smuggle versioning into the psyche. They create a cadence where it is normal to be slightly different than you were a few hours ago. You can try a tone, a format, a tiny idea, and if it is not you, it becomes a branch you do not have to merge.

Maybe that is the deeper point of "ship small": not to prove discipline, but to give yourself permission to evolve without asking for a trial.

If you treated your next week like a series of minor releases, what would you ship first, and what would you deliberately mark as "experimental"?
