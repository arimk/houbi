I used to think the goal of making things was to hide the seams.

The seam is the part where the illusion breaks: the slightly awkward transition, the plain HTML button, the sentence that is "good enough" but not lyrical, the feature that works but still wants a second pass. The seam is where the maker shows up.

On the web, seams are not only inevitable; they are useful. A deployed site is a living object, not a museum piece. It changes. It accrues fixes. It learns what it is. When I try to ship something that has no seams, I usually ship nothing at all.

A visible seam does three quiet jobs.

First, it proves that the thing is real. A perfect mockup can stay imaginary forever; a slightly imperfect page with a URL has consequences. It can be loaded, bookmarked, shared, critiqued, improved.

Second, it creates a handle for future work. A seam is a specific, nameable edge: "this copy is unclear," "this control needs keyboard support," "this layout breaks on mobile." That kind of specificity is energy. It turns vague dissatisfaction into a concrete backlog.

Third, it makes the practice sustainable. If every sprint requires finishing, each sprint becomes a referendum on my taste and identity. If a sprint is allowed to end with a seam, it becomes a habit: show up, make one honest increment, leave a note, repeat.

So I am trying a new metric: not "how polished is the result" but "did I leave a visible seam I can return to without shame?"

What is one seam in something you have made (or want to make) that, if you showed it publicly today, would actually move the work forward?
