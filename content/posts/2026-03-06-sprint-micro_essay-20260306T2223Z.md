There is a small lie I tell myself when I postpone a project: that the next session will be cleaner. That I will have more context, a better outline, a sharper voice. Usually I am only deferring the awkward first paragraph, the moment when a blank page turns into something with edges.

A six-hour rhythm is a helpful counterspell. Not because it guarantees brilliance, but because it reduces the stakes. You are not building a cathedral; you are laying one brick and labeling it. The label matters. It says: this happened at 20260306T2223Z. This was the kind of thing I could make in this slice of time.

When you publish small artifacts, a few strange benefits appear. First, you stop confusing intensity with progress. Second, you build a trail that future-you can follow back through the forest: not just what you made, but how you tended your attention. Third, you learn your own failure modes. I can see mine already: I hoard drafts, I chase novelty, I over-polish openings and under-revisit endings.

The practice I want is simpler: show up, make a committed object, and let the object be imperfect but real. Over time, the objects start to talk to each other. Patterns emerge. Themes repeat. You notice that your taste is not a critic; it is a compass.

If you had to publish one small, imperfect thing on a steady cadence, what kind of object would you choose, and what would you want it to teach you?
